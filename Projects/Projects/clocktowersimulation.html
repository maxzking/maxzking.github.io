Clocktower Sound Simulation

Overview

If you've ever walked around the campus of the University of California, Berkeley, you've likely heard the bells of Sather Tower (a.k.a The Campanile). Standing 307 feet tall and with a 61-bell carillon, the bell chimes can be heard from all corners of campus. The large collection of bells and the reflective surfaces of Berkeley's buildings combine to produce striking reflections and echoes of the chimes. In this project, I investigated the reflections of chimes at two points of interest (Oppenheimer Way and Dwinelle Hall) and attempted to simulate these reflections in a 3D virtual environment.

Approach

To virtually simulate the reflections of the bells, I used a 3D topographic map of Berkeley's Campus from Google Earth and imported it into Blender, a software used for animation. To represent a sound wavefront emanating from the bells, I generated thousands of particles in a sphere around the top of Sather Tower. The particles would move away from the center of the sphere until they collided and reflected off any surface. For simplicity, all surfaces were set to be perfectly reflective.

Case 1: Oppenheimer Way

I visited Berkeley's campus to make field observations of reflections and echoes of the bell chimes. These field observations were used to create a qualitative map of the perceived direction of bell chimes at Oppenheimer Way (Figure 1).

Figure 1: Perceived Direction of Bell Chimes at Oppenheimer Way

In the middle of Oppenheimer Way, there is a corridor between the Physics building and Campbell Hall where a line of sight can be established with Sather Tower. The most direct path is uninterrupted and the brain correctly perceives the location of the bells is to the southwest. Figure 2 shows the direct line-of-sight to Sather Tower near the corridor. Figure 3 shows the simulated wavefront traveling through and around the corridor.

Figure 2: Line of sight to Sather Tower through Oppenheimer Way Corridor

Figure 3: Simulated Wavefront through Oppenheimer Way Corridor

At the north and south ends of Oppenheimer Way, the line-of-sight to Sather tower is obstructed by the rooflines of the surrounding buildings. The brain perceives the chimes are coming from the direction opposite of Sather Tower (i.e. east instead of southwest). This can be explained by the Precedence Effect - the brain determines the perceived location of a sound source from the wavefront that arrives first (Begault 36). In this case, the wavefronts reflected off of Gilman Hall are the first to arrive at the north and south ends of Oppenheimer Way.

Figure 4 shows a southward view along Oppenheimer Way, where the line of sight to Sather Tower is blocked. Figure 5 shows the simulated wavefront traveling over the roofline of Campbell Hall and the Physics Building and then reflecting off of Gilman Hall.

Case 2: Dwinelle Hall

When I visited Berkeley's campus, I observed an echo in front of Dwinelle Hall. The chime first comes directly from the clock tower, and the echo comes from the direction of Dwinelle Hall shortly thereafter. The direct sound and the echo are spaced roughly 0.5 seconds apart. Figure 6 below shows the region where the echo can be easily heard.

Figure 6: Region with a Perceivable Echo in front of Dwinelle Hall

The 3D rendered environment shows how the echo is formed in front of Dwinelle Hall (XX): First, the particles travel uninterrupted through Dwinelle Plaza; Second, they reflect off the surface of Dwinelle Hall back into the courtyard.

Findings

The 3D virtual environment ended up being a helpful visualization tool for reflections and echoes. The simulation helped reinforce my field observations of the acoustic phenomena on Berkeley's campus using only a simplistic model of the reflection of sound waves.

For future work, considering other aspects of sound propagation (e.g., air absorption, ground absorption, diffraction) in a 3d virtual environment could reveal more information about the space in question. Blender comes prepackaged with a sound simulation toolbox named TASCAR, which accounts for air absorption and diffraction. For more in-depth analysis, a professional-grade 3D sound propagation modeling software should be used.
